# Figma — multiplayer collab in the browser killed Adobe's desktop monopoly

## Founding insight
Dylan Field and Evan Wallace saw that design was *inherently collaborative* — designers reviewed work with PMs, devs, and other designers constantly — yet every design tool (Sketch, Photoshop, Illustrator) was built for a solo user on a desktop sending files around. The contrarian bet: build a design tool in the browser so multiple people could be in the same canvas at once, even if it meant solving WebGL performance problems Adobe had decided weren't worth it.

## Initial wedge
Design teams at design-first product companies (early Airbnb, Coinbase, Robinhood, Brex) — the segment most willing to pay for designer happiness as a hiring signal. They had budget, design leadership, and acute pain from Sketch's "file-based" workflow with broken merges and dropped libraries.

## Timing call
2016. WebGL had matured enough to make a real-time vector editor in the browser viable; remote collaboration was rising; designers were already on Slack and Notion (browser-default tools). Two years earlier the browser stack wasn't there; two years later Sketch + Abstract + InVision would have tried to bolt collaboration onto a desktop file model.

## Distribution mechanism
Design community virality. Twitter showcases of "look at me Figma-ing with my team across 3 timezones." Designer-influencer ecosystem (Meng To, Pablo Stanley). Eventually the "view-only link" feature meant PMs and devs who didn't have Figma accounts saw it in every meeting — converted seats followed, organically expanding from the design team out.

## 10× moment
*Always-multiplayer*. Same dimension as Google Docs vs Microsoft Word — the file-based incumbents made you "share and merge"; Figma made you watch a cursor move in real time. That single dimension flipped the value of every other feature.

## Default-status moat
Once a design org migrated, leaving was unthinkable — every component library, every PM's Figma comment thread, every dev's Figma-to-code workflow was a switching cost. Adobe acquired Figma for $20B in 2022 (later abandoned for antitrust) — itself proof of the moat depth. A $20B price tag from the incumbent is the strongest possible signal you've built something they can't replicate.
