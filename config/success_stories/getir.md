# Getir — created the 10-minute-grocery category, then unit econ caught up

## Founding insight
Nazım Salur (Istanbul, 2015) saw that on-demand consumer expectations (taxis from Uber, food from delivery apps) had spread to groceries — people wanted "I forgot toothpaste" delivered in minutes, not next-day. The contrarian bet: pre-position inventory in micro-warehouses ("dark stores") inside dense urban neighborhoods, deliver in 10 minutes — turning groceries from a planned weekly trip into an on-demand impulse. Not faster shopping; a *different category*.

## Initial wedge
Urban Istanbul residents (dense neighborhoods, ~30 minute drive radius from a dark store) needing single-item top-ups: a forgotten ingredient mid-cooking, a baby diaper at 11pm, a craving. Not "the weekly shop" — explicitly the *small urgent order* that no incumbent could profitably serve.

## Timing call
2015 in Turkey, then 2020-2021 in Europe. Urban density + smartphone penetration + post-COVID delivery habits + cheap capital made the dark-store model economically viable for a window. Started in a constrained market (Turkey) which forced disciplined unit economics from the start — then international scale in 2021 came with capital that distorted those constraints.

## Distribution mechanism
Hyperlocal launch strategy: pick one dense neighborhood, open one dark store, advertise hard locally (paper flyers + Instagram), reach 30% of households fast, then move to the next neighborhood. Founder personally lived in target districts and saw what worked. International expansion (Berlin, Amsterdam, London, NYC) repeated the playbook.

## 10× moment
*Speed*. 10-minute groceries vs hours from Instacart or planned weekly trips. On the time-to-delivery dimension, the gap was unmistakable — and once people experienced it, normal grocery shopping felt slow. The category itself was new.

## Default-status moat — and its limits
By 2021 Getir was the verb in Istanbul for instant groceries. But the global expansion (10 countries, $12B valuation) tested whether dark-store economics worked outside dense Turkish cities. They didn't — labor costs in Western Europe + density requirements made unit economics fragile. 2022-2024: pull back from most markets, layoffs, return to Turkey-focused. **Lesson:** creating a category is real and durable in the right geography; *scaling unit economics across geographies* is a separate problem that doesn't transfer with the playbook. The win shape was right; the global cap was lower than capital assumed.
