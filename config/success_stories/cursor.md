# Cursor — forked VS Code, beat Copilot via tighter UX loop

## Founding insight
Michael Truell and the founding team saw that GitHub Copilot was a *plugin* (autocomplete inside someone else's IDE) — meaning it couldn't change the entire interaction model. The contrarian bet: fork VS Code itself so the AI could see the full codebase, edit multiple files, and run agentic flows that a plugin architecturally couldn't. Trade off the distribution penalty of being a separate app for the freedom to redefine the loop.

## Initial wedge
Power developers — engineers at YC startups, AI labs, and indie tool-makers who were already paying for ChatGPT Plus and looking for "AI in my actual workflow." They didn't need convincing on the value of AI coding; they needed a 10× tool. Not "everyone who codes" — specifically the segment that would pay $20/month for a better loop.

## Timing call
2023, post-GPT-4. The model was finally capable enough to do multi-file edits and agentic flows reliably. Six months earlier the model couldn't carry the experience; later, GitHub would seriously start moving on Copilot Workspace. The window between "AI coding is real" and "Microsoft fully reacts" was the asymmetric opportunity, and Cursor compressed two years of product development into that window.

## Distribution mechanism
Developer Twitter + YouTube demo virality. Every release demoed something Copilot couldn't do — multi-file refactor, terminal-aware agentic editing, codebase chat. Pure organic, no paid. By the time competitors caught up, the brand had hardened — "I'm a Cursor user" became identity signaling among AI-forward engineers.

## 10× moment
*Multi-file context + agentic editing*. Copilot was line-level autocomplete; Cursor was "describe a change and watch it span the codebase." On the dimension that matters most for senior engineers (architectural changes, not snippet completion), the gap was a step-change.

## Default-status moat
Speed of iteration vs Microsoft. Cursor shipped weekly; GitHub ships quarterly. By the time Microsoft's Copilot Workspace had multi-file edits, Cursor had agentic terminals, codebase-aware chat, and a power-user community whose workflows were already wired in. Incumbent reaction time was the moat — temporary but renewable while the team kept shipping faster than the giant could pivot. The lesson: incumbent inertia, not technical novelty, is often the durable advantage.
