# Stripe — "two lines of code" wedge into a $300B saturated payments market

## Founding insight
Patrick and John Collison saw that payments weren't broken for merchants — they were broken for *developers*. Every existing solution (Authorize.net, PayPal Business) required business contracts, compliance review, and gateway integration that took weeks. The contrarian bet: developers, given a clean API, would build the products that needed payments, and they'd carry their merchants with them. Incumbents were optimizing for enterprise procurement; nobody was selling to the engineer.

## Initial wedge
YC startups and indie hackers building "make money on the internet" projects — the exact people existing processors actively rejected or stalled. Stripe approved instantly and shipped working integrations in hours. The wedge wasn't a segment by *size* but a segment by *frustration*. These users had nowhere else to go.

## Timing call
2010-2011. Cloud + smartphones had created a flood of new internet businesses that needed payments, while card networks and incumbents (PayPal) were focused on enterprise. The dev-tools generation (Heroku, GitHub, AWS) had normalized "infrastructure as a library you import." Three years earlier the audience for an API-first payments tool didn't exist; three years later it would have been competed for.

## Distribution mechanism
Pure developer word-of-mouth. Hacker News, Reddit, Twitter. No marketing team for the first three years. When the integration is shorter than the blog post about it, the blog post itself becomes the distribution. Stripe Press (later) institutionalized this — high-craft writing as a brand asset.

## 10× moment
The integration was 10× *faster*, not 10× cheaper. Minutes vs. weeks. Other dimensions (price, features) were comparable or worse — that didn't matter. Friction reduction along one dimension at a step-change scale carried the entire product.

## Default-status moat
Once they crossed ~$50B in annual payment volume around 2017, every new internet business defaulted to "we'll use Stripe" before evaluating alternatives. Network effects: developer mindshare + integration depth across thousands of long-tail tools (Shopify, Substack, Lyft) meant Stripe was already wired in everywhere a new entrepreneur would look. Switching cost compounded with every revenue-paid month.
