# Discord — gaming-first wedge, ten-year compound into community + work

## Founding insight
Stan Vishnevskiy and Jason Citron saw that gamers needed three things in one tool: voice that didn't degrade mid-raid, persistent text channels, and free server hosting. Existing options forced bad choices (TeamSpeak required a server admin, Skype was for adults, Slack was paid + work-coded). The contrarian bet: build a free product specifically for gamers — and once they had millions of daily users in a sticky social product, the same architecture would carry community/work later.

## Initial wedge
Hardcore PC gamers in League of Legends, World of Warcraft, and Counter-Strike clans — exactly the users who would tolerate beta bugs and tell their friends. Discord shipped with gamer-first defaults (push-to-talk, low-latency voice, free unlimited rooms) that no business-oriented tool would prioritize.

## Timing call
2015. Esports was exploding, streaming on Twitch had normalized "watching games is a thing," and existing voice tools (Skype, Mumble) hadn't adapted. Three years earlier the gamer base wasn't big enough to support a venture-scale company; three years later Slack would have considered gaming verticals.

## Distribution mechanism
Game-by-game community capture. Discord seeded a server for every major game's community, partnered with streamers and esports orgs, and let users invite their friends with a single link. The product's NETWORK was the marketing — once your clan was on Discord, you came back daily.

## 10× moment
*Voice quality + free server hosting*. TeamSpeak charged $30/year for a server; Discord was free with better audio compression and zero ops. On the two dimensions gamers cared most about, the gap was unmistakable — and pricing at zero meant communities switched as a group, not one user at a time.

## Default-status moat
By 2020 Discord was the default community platform for not just gaming but crypto, AI, indie hackers, and study groups — same product, multiple markets. The decade-long compound: a single product built for one niche kept attracting new niches as voice + persistent community became the universal pattern. Microsoft tried to acquire for $12B in 2021; Discord said no — meaning they believed the compound wasn't over.
